System temperature (Tsys)
=========================

System temperature (Tsys) is the total noise power in a radio telescope receiver system expressed as an equivalent temperature.
Radio astronomers use temperature units because noise power from a resistor or receiver follows thermal statistics described by the Boltzmann relation.
Tsys represents the sum of all noise contributions entering the receiver. 📡
A common approximation:
Tsys = Trec + Tant
Where
Trec = receiver noise temperature from electronics
Tant = antenna temperature from the sky and environment
In practice the antenna term contains several components:
Tsys = Trec + Tsky + Tatm + Tspill + Tcmb + Trfi
Typical terms
Trec
Noise generated by amplifiers, mixers, and electronics.
Tsky
Emission from astronomical sources and the Galactic background.
Tatm
Atmospheric emission from oxygen and water vapor.
Tspill
Ground radiation entering through sidelobes or spillover.
Tcmb
Cosmic microwave background, about 2.725 K.
Trfi
Radio frequency interference. Often variable.
So Tsys represents the total noise seen by the detector.
Why Tsys matters
Tsys sets the noise level of the measurement.
The sensitivity of a radio telescope improves when:
Tsys decreases
bandwidth increases
integration time increases
Lower Tsys means better sensitivity.
How Tsys is measured
Single-dish telescopes usually measure Tsys using calibration loads.
Common methods:
Noise diode injection
Hot–cold load calibration
Sky tipping scans
The noise diode method is widely used in monitoring observations.
Limitations of Tsys
Tsys is useful but imperfect. ⚠️
1. It assumes noise is thermal and Gaussian
The radiometer equation assumes random thermal noise.
Real observations often include:
RFI
gain fluctuations
instrumental instabilities
These produce noise that does not behave like radiometer noise.
Result: measured RMS larger than predicted.
2. Tsys changes with elevation
Atmospheric emission increases at low elevation.
Higher airmass → higher Tsys.
If not corrected, sensitivity estimates become inaccurate.
3. It does not describe gain variations
Receiver gain drift changes measured signal strength but may not change Tsys strongly.
Effects include
temperature drift in electronics
power supply variation
amplifier instability
These create systematic errors, not thermal noise.
4. It ignores pointing errors
If the telescope is slightly mispointed:
source signal decreases
Tsys remains similar
This produces incorrect flux measurements even though Tsys looks normal.
5. Tsys does not include calibration errors
Flux calibration depends on
gain curves
beam efficiency
atmospheric opacity corrections
Tsys alone does not account for these effects.
6. Strong sources distort Tsys
For very bright sources
the antenna temperature contributes significantly to Tsys
receiver response may become nonlinear
This violates the assumptions behind the radiometer equation.
Practical interpretation in your drift-scan work
When you compute the RMS of the baseline in a scan:
If RMS ≈ radiometer prediction from Tsys
→ system behaves normally.
If RMS > predicted
→ additional noise sources exist.
Typical causes:
RFI
weather fluctuations
gain instability
baseline ripple
Key idea
Tsys measures how noisy the telescope system is, but it does not guarantee the data quality.
Good observations require:
low Tsys
stable gain
clean spectrum
good pointing
minimal RFI
All must work together. 📡
